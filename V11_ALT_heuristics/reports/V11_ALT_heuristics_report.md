# V11_ALT_heuristics — Lead-Time Heuristics vs V10.6.2

_Generated by V11_ALT_heuristics_compare. Honest gate: within-truck z>=2 AND outside NF p05-p95, MIN_EO_SAMPLES=200._

## Executive Summary

**Bottom line.** Implementing all 12 candidate heuristics on the same 6 raw CAN
channels raises discriminative-precursor recall to **6/10** (vs the frozen
V10.6.2 baseline of **5/10**) with **0/15 NF false alarms**, and produces an
**earlier** first warning for one truck. The result is fully reproducible (the
orchestrator rebuilds it deterministically end to end). This is a real but
**modest** gain — it does not overturn the project's standing finding that most
alternator failures are abrupt/silent with no electrical precursor.

**What actually changed (2 trucks):**
- **VIN9** — newly detected at the 30-day horizon by `crank_recovery_t`
  (post-crank voltage recovery, heuristic #3). No V10.6.2 channel saw it.
- **VIN1** — earliest warning moved from **30d → 60d**, also via
  `crank_recovery_t`, i.e. an earlier lead than the previous GED=2 signal.
- The other 8 trucks are unchanged: 4 were already detected (VIN2/6/8/10) and
  4 remain undetected by any channel (VIN3/4/5/7).

**The MVP heuristic: #3 post-crank recovery (`crank_recovery_t`).** It is
discriminative on 6/10 failed VINs with 0/15 NF false alarms and is the only new
feature that moved the headline recall. This validates the source document's
Tier-1 hypothesis that "every key-on is a free stress test" — a weakening
charging/battery interface recovers to 27 V more slowly after the crank load is
removed.

**Honest caveats (do not over-read):**
1. `crank_recovery_t`'s within-truck z-scores are very large (VIN8 ~1288,
   VIN9 ~155). These are inflated by near-zero baseline recovery-time variance
   plus the 30 s recovery-window censor — read them as "recovery clearly slowed
   vs the healthy fleet," **not** as a calibrated effect size. The trustworthy
   guard is the 0/15 NF self-test, which the feature passes.
2. **VIN8**'s strict-gate detection rests on only **3 trusted days** in the final
   30 days (`n_days_final_30d=3`) — fragile. The compound alarm (below) catches
   VIN8 earlier (@90d) and more robustly.
3. The **compound 2-of-5 weak-vote alarm (#11)** fires on **4/10** failed trucks
   with **0/15** NF false alarms, and gives the *earliest* first-trigger for some
   (VIN8 @90d, VIN1 @60d). It is a useful orthogonal "early-watch" tier even
   though its recall is lower than the strict gate.
4. **Change-point (#12)** fires far too early to be actionable (lead times
   220–599 days) — treat as exploratory only. **Resting-slope (#6)** is
   discriminative on 3/10 (VIN1/4/10).
5. "Generalizes" in the table below = discriminative in ≥2 failed trucks in
   *some* horizon window with 0–1 NF false alarms. That is a **coverage /
   fault-typing** signal (useful for repair guidance — e.g. high-load vs idle sag
   typing), **weaker** than "drove the earliest-precursor verdict." Only
   `crank_recovery_t` actually changed the headline recall.

**Recommended for the deployment emergency channel:** add `crank_recovery_t`
alongside the existing GED=2 storm signal, and surface the compound 2-of-5 vote
as an "early-watch" tier. The fault-typing features (`sag_highload_frac` vs
`sag_idle_frac`, `reg_duty_frac`) add repair-direction value even where they do
not change recall. The deliverable shape is unchanged: WHICH (classifier) +
WHEN-fleet (Weibull) + WHEN-emergency (now firing earlier for VIN1 and for one
additional truck, VIN9).
